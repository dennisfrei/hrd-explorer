Reading the diagram

The Hertzsprung–Russell diagram plots a star's luminosity against its surface temperature. By convention the temperature axis is inverted — hot, blue stars on the left, cool, red stars on the right. The vertical axis is luminosity on a logarithmic scale, log(L / L☉), spanning faint white dwarfs at the bottom to luminous supergiants at the top.

Interactive — how the parameters connect

Drag the two sliders. They set the only independent quantities — surface temperature and luminosity, the diagram's own axes — and everything else follows: the radius from Stefan–Boltzmann, the peak wavelength and colour from Wien's law, the spectral class, and (on the main sequence) the mass and age.

Radius — Stefan–Boltzmann law

A star radiates as a blackbody, so its luminosity is set by its surface area and temperature. Solving for the radius:

RR⊙ = LL⊙ · (T⊙Teff)2

with T☉ = 5778 K. This is why the radius isolines run diagonally: at fixed radius, a hotter star is more luminous. In log–log space the lines have a slope of +4, because L ∝ R² T_eff⁴.

Peak wavelength — Wien's law

The wavelength at which a blackbody emits most strongly depends only on temperature:

λmax= 2.898×106Teff nm

Hot stars peak in the ultraviolet and appear blue; cool stars peak in the red and infrared. This drives the colour sequence shown by the dots and the optional colour background.

Spectral class & colour

Stars are binned by temperature into the classes O B A F G K M (hottest to coolest). The colours used here follow a blackbody sequence that is monotonic from red through white to blue — a physical constraint, never an arbitrary palette.

The Main Sequence & mass

Stars fusing hydrogen in their cores lie along the Zero-Age Main Sequence (ZAMS), drawn here from Ekström et al. (2012). Along this band, mass increases monotonically with temperature and luminosity. Mass is shown only on the ZAMS, because off the main sequence a star's mass is not determined by its position (T_eff, L) alone.

Main-sequence lifetime

More massive stars are far more luminous and burn their fuel much faster. The time spent on the main sequence scales steeply with mass:

τMS≈ 1010· (MM⊙)−2.5 yr

A 10 M☉ star lives only a few million years; a 0.5 M☉ star outlives the present age of the Universe.

Estimated age

As a star burns core hydrogen it slowly brightens, drifting upward from the ZAMS line toward the Terminal-Age Main Sequence. We use that vertical offset to estimate how much of the main-sequence lifetime has elapsed:

age≈f·τMS , f=clamp( logL−logLZAMS0.55 ,0,1)

This is a simplified teaching estimate, not an isochrone fit: a star exactly on the ZAMS reads as essentially age zero, one near the top of the band as near τ_MS. Age is reported only on the main sequence, where the model applies.

Luminosity classes

At a given temperature, more luminous stars are larger. The classes run from V (main-sequence dwarfs) through IV (subgiants), III (giants), and II–I (bright giants and supergiants), with VII for the compact, faint white dwarfs below the main sequence.
