Reading the diagram

The Hertzsprung–Russell diagram plots a star's luminosity against its surface temperature. By convention the temperature axis is inverted — hot, blue stars on the left, cool, red stars on the right. The vertical axis is luminosity on a logarithmic scale, log(L / L☉), spanning faint white dwarfs at the bottom to luminous supergiants at the top.

Interactive — how the parameters connect

Drag the two sliders. They set the only independent quantities — surface temperature and luminosity, the diagram's own axes — and everything else follows: the radius from Stefan–Boltzmann, the peak wavelength and colour from Wien's law, the spectral class, and (on the main sequence) the mass and age.

Radius — Stefan–Boltzmann law

A star radiates as a blackbody, so its luminosity is set by its surface area and temperature. Solving for the radius:

RR⊙ = LL⊙ · (T⊙Teff)2

with T☉ = 5778 K. This is why the radius isolines run diagonally: at fixed radius, a hotter star is more luminous. In log–log space the lines have a slope of +4, because L ∝ R² T_eff⁴.

Peak wavelength — Wien's law

The wavelength at which a blackbody emits most strongly depends only on temperature:

λmax= 2.898×106Teff nm

Hot stars peak in the ultraviolet and appear blue; cool stars peak in the red and infrared. This drives the colour sequence shown by the dots and the optional colour background.

Blackbody radiation & the Planck spectrum

A star's photosphere is dense enough that radiation and matter are nearly in thermal equilibrium, so the emergent spectrum is close to that of an ideal blackbody at the effective temperature. The full spectral radiance follows Planck's law:

Bλ(T)= 2hc2λ5 1ehc/λkBT−1

This single curve ties the tool together: differentiating it gives Wien's law (the peak you see marked in the interactive spectrum), and integrating it over all wavelengths and solid angle gives the total emergent flux ∝T4 — the Stefan–Boltzmann law. Real spectra depart from a pure Planck curve: absorption lines, bound–free edges such as the Balmer jump, and limb darkening all imprint structure. The blackbody is the correct zeroth-order picture and the basis for the colours used here.

LTE, NLTE & the Stefan–Boltzmann law

Treating the emergent spectrum as a blackbody assumes Local Thermodynamic Equilibrium (LTE): at each depth the gas is described by a single temperature, atomic level populations follow the Boltzmann and Saha distributions, and the source function equals the Planck function. LTE holds well in the dense, collision-dominated deep photosphere.

In hot stars, in the thin atmospheres of giants, and within strong spectral lines, radiative transition rates outpace collisions and the level populations decouple from the local temperature. This is the Non-LTE (NLTE) regime, where the source function must be solved self-consistently with the radiation field rather than set equal to Bλ.

The effective temperature is defined through the bolometric luminosity so that it remains meaningful regardless:

L=4πR2σTeff4

Teff is the temperature of the blackbody that radiates the same total flux per unit surface area as the star. It is exactly this relation, inverted, that the diagram uses to draw the radius isolines.

Spectral class & colour

Stars are binned by temperature into the classes O B A F G K M (hottest to coolest). The colours used here follow a blackbody sequence that is monotonic from red through white to blue — a physical constraint, never an arbitrary palette.

The Main Sequence & mass

Stars fusing hydrogen in their cores lie along the Zero-Age Main Sequence (ZAMS), drawn here from Ekström et al. (2012). Along this band, mass increases monotonically with temperature and luminosity. Mass is shown only on the ZAMS, because off the main sequence a star's mass is not determined by its position (T_eff, L) alone.

Main-sequence lifetime

More massive stars are far more luminous and burn their fuel much faster. The time spent on the main sequence scales steeply with mass:

τMS≈ 1010· (MM⊙)−2.5 yr

A 10 M☉ star lives only a few million years; a 0.5 M☉ star outlives the present age of the Universe.

Estimated age

As a star burns core hydrogen it slowly brightens, drifting upward from the ZAMS line toward the Terminal-Age Main Sequence. We use that vertical offset to estimate how much of the main-sequence lifetime has elapsed:

age≈f·τMS , f=clamp( logL−logLZAMS0.55 ,0,1)

This is a simplified teaching estimate, not an isochrone fit: a star exactly on the ZAMS reads as essentially age zero, one near the top of the band as near τ_MS. Age is reported only on the main sequence, where the model applies.

Stellar evolution — why L and R change

A star's place in the diagram is fixed by just two quantities, its luminosity and its radius, and both evolve as the interior changes — so a track across the HR diagram is a star's life story.

On the main sequence, core hydrogen fusion converts H into He, raising the mean molecular weight. The core slowly contracts and heats, fusion runs a little faster, and the star gradually brightens and expands — the upward drift away from the ZAMS that this tool turns into an age estimate.

When core hydrogen runs out, the inert helium core contracts and a hydrogen-burning shell ignites around it. The envelope expands enormously and cools: the star moves right and up onto the red-giant branch, growing to tens or hundreds of solar radii at roughly constant — then rising — luminosity. Here radius changes dominate the motion in the diagram.

Later stages — core helium burning, shell flashes, and for massive stars successive heavier-element burning — sweep the star across the upper diagram before its endpoint as a white dwarf, neutron star, or black hole. Throughout, the same two levers, the energy-generation rate (luminosity) and the radius set by the envelope's structure, decide where the star sits.

Luminosity classes

At a given temperature, more luminous stars are larger. The classes run from V (main-sequence dwarfs) through IV (subgiants), III (giants), and II–I (bright giants and supergiants), with VII for the compact, faint white dwarfs below the main sequence.
